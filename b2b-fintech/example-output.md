## Persona: "The Moderniser Under Pressure"

**Name:** Marcus Tan (fictional)
**Title:** VP, Payments Modernisation
**Company:** Regional bank, ~8,000 employees, headquartered in Singapore
**One-line summary:** A technically fluent payments leader trying to move a slow-moving institution fast enough to stay competitive — without enough budget, buy-in, or time.

---

## Role & Responsibilities

Marcus owns the bank's payments infrastructure roadmap. His team of 12 is responsible for evaluating new rails (ISO 20022, real-time payments, cross-border corridors), managing vendor relationships, and delivering modernisation projects that typically run 18–36 months.

He reports to the Chief Technology Officer and presents quarterly to the CFO on project ROI. He influences purchasing decisions significantly but rarely holds final budget sign-off — that sits one level above him.

**How success is measured:**
- On-time delivery of modernisation milestones
- Cost reduction in payment processing per transaction
- Reduction in settlement failures and reconciliation errors
- Internal stakeholder satisfaction (which is harder to measure and often more painful)

**Where he sits in buying decisions:** Key influencer and primary evaluator. He builds the business case; his CTO approves it.

---

## Pain Points & Motivations

**Top 3 professional pain points:**

1. **Legacy systems that resist change** — Core banking infrastructure is 15–20 years old. Every modernisation project hits unexpected dependencies. He's constantly told "we can't touch that system" by teams who don't want accountability for what breaks.

2. **Proving ROI before the project is done** — His CFO wants financial returns before Marcus can even complete a pilot. The pressure to justify investment in infrastructure — which doesn't produce visible revenue — is constant and exhausting.

3. **Vendor promises vs. vendor reality** — He's been burned by vendors who oversold capabilities and underdelivered on implementation. He now spends as much time managing vendor risk as he does on actual strategy.

**The deeper fear:**
That he'll spend 18 months on a major modernisation project, it'll ship late, over budget, or with limited adoption — and his career progression will stall as a result. He's seen it happen to peers.

**What success looks like in 12 months:**
One major infrastructure milestone delivered on time, a cross-border payment corridor live with measurable cost reduction, and enough internal credibility to get the next project funded without a battle.

**The language he actually uses:**
> "We're stuck in a cycle of technical debt."
> "I need to show quick wins to keep the programme funded."
> "Our vendors promise enterprise-grade but deliver SME-quality support."
> "I can build the business case — I just need the right data to back it up."

---

## Content & Channel Behaviour

**Where he consumes content:**
- LinkedIn (10–15 mins daily, mostly scrolling on mobile between meetings)
- Industry newsletters: The Paypers, Finextra, PYMNTS
- Analyst reports: McKinsey, BCG, occasionally Gartner when evaluating vendors
- Conferences: attends 1–2 per year, prioritises ones where he can hear peers, not vendors

**Formats he trusts most:**
- Case studies with real implementation data (not "up to X% improvement" — actual numbers)
- Peer panels at industry events — hearing how someone at a comparable bank solved the same problem
- Analyst research that validates his existing thinking or challenges it with evidence

**Who influences his thinking:**
- Peers at other banks (most trusted — what are other VPs actually doing?)
- Industry analysts at McKinsey, Oliver Wyman, BIS
- Regulators — MAS guidance, BIS innovation hub papers shape his risk framing
- Not vendors — he discounts vendor-produced content heavily

**What would make him stop scrolling:**
A LinkedIn post that names a specific problem he's living with right now — ideally from a peer, not a brand. Something like: *"Why most payments modernisation programmes stall at year two — and what the ones that don't have in common."*

---

## Buying Triggers & Objections

**What creates urgency:**
- A regulatory deadline (e.g. MAS mandating ISO 20022 compliance by a specific date)
- A competitor launching a capability he doesn't have
- A board-level directive to "become more digital" that lands without a budget or a plan
- His annual review cycle — Q4 is when he builds the following year's business cases

**His evaluation process:**
- Initial research: 2–4 weeks of desk research and peer conversations
- Shortlisting: builds a comparison matrix, involves his procurement team
- Final decision: requires CTO sign-off; sometimes a CFO presentation
- Timeline from awareness to decision: 3–6 months for significant purchases; 6–12 for major infrastructure contracts

**Top 3 objections:**

1. *"Is the content actually senior enough for me?"* — He's attended events where the panels were too introductory or too vendor-led. He needs to know there are peers at his level on stage, not just vendors pitching.

2. *"Can I justify the time out of office?"* — A 3-day conference in Bangkok requires business case justification to his CTO. He needs to come back with something — a contact, an insight, a decision made.

3. *"I've seen this before and nothing changed."* — Scepticism about whether an event will surface genuinely new thinking vs. recycled content he's already read.

**What would make him say yes faster:**
- Seeing a specific speaker or session agenda item that maps directly to his current project challenge
- A peer at a comparable bank telling him it was worth attending last year
- A clear agenda with named speakers before he has to commit

**What would kill it:**
A speaker lineup dominated by vendors. A registration process that requires him to talk to sales. Any messaging that feels like it's targeting a junior audience.

## Marketing Implications

**Best channel:** LinkedIn (sponsored content + InMail), followed by Finextra and The Paypers newsletter placements

**Best message angle:** Peer validation + agenda specificity. Don't sell the event — sell the specific problem it helps him solve and the specific peers he'll be in the room with.

**What NOT to say:**
- "Asia's biggest fintech event" — scale doesn't reassure him; relevance does
- "Networking opportunities" — too vague; he needs to know *who* he's networking with
- Generic urgency like "Don't miss out" — he's immune to it

**Content he would actually share with colleagues:**
A well-argued article on why payments modernisation programmes fail at year two — especially if it included data from APAC banks. He'd forward it to his CTO with a note: *"Worth reading before our Q3 planning session."*
