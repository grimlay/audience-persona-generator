## Persona: "The Informed Procrastinator"

**Name:** Priya Nair (fictional)
**Age:** 28
**Job:** Marketing executive at a tech company, SGD 5,500/month
**One-line summary:** She knows she should be investing. She's been meaning to start for two years. She follows four finance influencers and still hasn't opened an account.

---

## Financial Profile

**Products currently used:**
- DBS savings account (salary account, barely earns interest)
- One credit card (paid in full monthly — she's proud of this)
- CPF (knows it exists, doesn't really think about it)
- Has downloaded two investment apps in the past 18 months, never completed onboarding

**Financial literacy level:** Medium — she understands concepts like compound interest, diversification, and ETFs at a headline level. She could explain what a robo-advisor is. She cannot confidently explain what she should actually invest in or how much.

**Relationship with money:** Anxious but engaged. Money is something she thinks about a lot but acts on infrequently. She saves consistently but keeps too much in cash because it feels "safe." Investing feels like a commitment she might get wrong.

**Primary financial goal right now:** Start investing — specifically, get money out of a low-interest savings account and into something that works harder. Vaguely aware she should also be thinking about property in the next 3–5 years.

---

## Pain Points & Motivations

**Top 3 financial pain points:**

1. **Paralysis from too much information** — She's watched dozens of videos on ETFs, robo-advisors, individual stocks, and REITs. Every video makes a slightly different argument. She doesn't know who to trust or where to start, so she doesn't start.

2. **Fear of making the wrong first move** — She's not afraid of losing all her money — she's afraid of making a decision she'll regret and feel embarrassed about. Getting it wrong feels more costly than staying in cash.

3. **Not knowing how much is "enough" to start** — She has SGD 15,000 saved. She doesn't know if she should invest SGD 1,000, SGD 5,000, or all of it. The uncertainty about the right amount keeps her from committing to any amount.

**The specific fear:**
That she'll finally start, the market will drop, and she'll feel like she was naive for listening to influencers. She doesn't want to be the person who bought at the top.

**What she wants in 12 months:**
To have started. That's genuinely it. She wants to be able to say she has an investment portfolio, even a small one — and to feel like she understands what's in it.

**The language she actually uses:**
> "I keep saying I'll do it when I have more time to figure it out."
> "I don't want to just throw money in and not know what I'm doing."
> "My friend uses [app] but I don't know if that's right for me."
> "I have money sitting in my savings account doing nothing and it stresses me out."

## Content & Channel Behaviour

**Where she consumes financial content:**
- TikTok (daily — discovers new concepts here, short bursts)
- Instagram (follows 4–5 personal finance accounts, saves posts to "finance" highlight)
- YouTube (longer deep-dives when she's already interested in a topic — weekend viewing)
- Reddit r/singaporefi (lurks, rarely posts — reads threads when she has a specific question)
- Does not read financial news sites — finds them too dense and aimed at people who already know what they're doing

**Formats she trusts:**
- Peer reviews in app stores — she reads 20+ reviews before downloading anything financial
- "I tried this for 6 months" style creator content — personal experience over theoretical advice
- Friends who have actually done it — one friend's recommendation outweighs ten influencer posts

**Who influences her financial decisions:**
- Two or three TikTok/Instagram creators she's followed for over a year and trusts personally (not just financially)
- Friends who are slightly ahead of her financially and seem to have it figured out
- Notably not: her bank, financial advisors, or any brand-produced content she can identify as an ad

**What would make her stop scrolling:**
Specific, relatable language about her exact situation. Headlines like *"Still haven't started investing? Here's the actual reason why (it's not laziness)"* or *"How I invested my first SGD 1,000 when I had no idea what I was doing."* She responds to honesty about confusion — not aspirational wealth content.

## Buying Triggers & Objections

**What creates openness to trying a new product:**
- A friend mentions they've been using an app for a few months and it's going well
- A TikTok creator she already trusts does a walkthrough of a specific app (not a paid ad — an organic recommendation)
- A moment of frustration with her savings account interest rate — usually when she checks her balance and does the maths
- End-of-year reflection — January is her highest intent period

**How she evaluates a new app:**
- Checks App Store rating and reads negative reviews first (what are the complaints?)
- Looks for a "how it works" explainer that doesn't require her to already understand investing
- Checks if there's a minimum investment amount — she wants to start small
- Looks to see if anyone she follows has mentioned it

**Top 3 objections:**

1. *"What if I lose money and regret it?"* — Not fear of poverty — fear of a bad decision she'll have to explain to herself.

2. *"I don't understand what I'd actually be investing in."* — She needs to feel like she understands the product before she trusts it with her money. Black-box investing feels unsafe even if it's technically simpler.

3. *"Is this app actually legit?"* — Singapore has MAS licensing but she doesn't check that. She uses social proof as a proxy for legitimacy: app store ratings, number of users, whether people she knows use it.

**What reassures her:**
- MAS-regulated badge (she may not know exactly what it means but it signals legitimacy)
- "Start from SGD 1" or similarly low minimums — removes the "how much is enough" paralysis
- A simple, jargon-free onboarding that explains what she's investing in and why
- Seeing a real portfolio example with realistic (not extraordinary) returns

**What would make her churn:**
An interface that requires financial knowledge she doesn't have. Jargon without explanation. A pushy upsell to a premium plan before she's even made her first investment.

---

## Marketing Implications

**Best channel:** TikTok and Instagram — organic-style content, not polished brand ads. Creator partnerships with mid-tier finance influencers she already follows outperform any direct brand campaign.

**Best message angle:** Meet her where she is — not "start building wealth today" but "it's okay that you haven't started yet, here's the simplest first step." Validation before aspiration.

**What NOT to say:**
- Anything implying she should already know this ("as any savvy investor knows...")
- Return-focused headlines ("grow your money by X%") — triggers scepticism, not excitement
- Urgency that feels manufactured ("limited time offer") — she sees through it immediately

**The one thing that would make her recommend it to a friend:**
If the onboarding is genuinely simple and she feels smart — not overwhelmed — after completing it. She'll screenshot her first portfolio and send it to her group chat. That's the moment. Make onboarding feel like an achievement, not a form-fill.
